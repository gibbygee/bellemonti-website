The deaths look different but the underlying pattern is consistent. Lost coherence. The metric replaces the mission.

## Efficiency theater

The most common death. A company needs a turnaround, efficiency is the obvious lever. So you optimize each cylinder of the V8 individually - sales gets its playbook, dev gets its process, product gets its framework. Each function locally optimized. The sum performs worse than its parts. Strategic coherence disappears because no one is in charge of the whole engine.

## The best operators

The careerists tend to "win the climb." They know how to read the room, manage up, hit the number, package the slide. That's the skill set that gets you to the top of a big company, and it's a real skill set, for sure. Most of the people running things got there because they were genuinely good at it.

The problem is that the skill set that wins the climb is not the skill set that runs the company once you're there. What got you here won't get you there. At the top, the optics no longer have anyone to play to. Managing up doesn't work when there's no one above you. The metric is something you set, not something you hit.

And the muscle memory is hard to drop. Operators who climbed by managing for optics keep managing for optics. They keep the deck tight. They keep the metric green. They stop deeply understanding the user, because deeply understanding the user was never what got them promoted. Growth dies quietly while the org runs beautifully on paper.

The best operators are the ones willing to un-learn the climb. To forget the **ladder behaviors** that worked for two decades and pick up a different set: bold bets, creative judgment, sitting with a customer, being wrong in public, killing your own pet project. Leadership matters here in a way it doesn't lower in the org, because at the top there's no process to hide behind.
