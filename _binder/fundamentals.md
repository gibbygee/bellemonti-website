  <cite>Bruce Lee</cite>
</div>

Bruce Lee is the sharpest statement of an idea that shows up everywhere: depth over breadth, fundamentals over novelty, repetition over variety.

In PM: what differentiates great PMs from good ones isn't the frameworks. It's that great PMs listen to what customers don't say and anticipate where the industry is going. That's not a process. That's earned through repetition. Thousands of customer conversations. Thousands of prioritization decisions. The judgment compounds.

In hard tech: manufacturing skills take decades. You can't shortcut the execution layer. Monozukuri — the art of making — is tacit knowledge. It lives in hands, not manuals. SaaS could be spun up by a team in a weekend. A precision machining capability takes a generation to build. The defensibility is in the difficulty of accumulation.

## Where fundamentals hold the line

- **Shinise companies** — Japanese businesses that survive for centuries do it by committing to "hosoku nagaku" — thin and long. Stability and longevity matter more than short-term dynamism.
- **The software trap** — Companies in trouble usually aren't the ones that stopped innovating. They're the ones that let the fundamentals erode — maintenance deprioritized, the whole product neglected (support, docs, demos), the customer feedback loop broken down.
- **The inertia insight** — Many companies succeed in spite of themselves. Nobody wants to focus on the fundamentals. That's the moat.

Innovation on a crumbling foundation isn't innovation. It's decoration. Boring things, done well, for a long time.
