---
title: As good as it gets
---

Most discourse about software companies assumes a growth-stage context. Most companies are not there. Many are in maturity or decline. The product lifecycle is real, and maturity can last 15 years. Go ask IBM about the AS/400.

## The cargo cult problem

Mature companies try to model the operating system of high-growth companies, believing the imitation will produce the same results. It won't.

The phrase "we need to do this the RIGHT way" is a red flag. There is no right way other than the way that makes the company more successful.

## Build the machine

For mature products, "as good as it gets" is a repeatable system. Control surfaces.

1. A way to reliably set priorities.
2. A way to put those priorities into development.
3. Some predictability on when things will emerge.
4. Confidence that what ships will be marketable and useful.
5. A feedback loop back to step 1.

Extending that timeline is very lucrative. Don't chase the platonic ideal. Build the machine.

## What changes when you accept maturity

- **Stop reorganizing.** The org chart isn't the bottleneck. It rarely is. Strategic incoherence is.
- **Stop chasing growth-stage playbooks.** Different mode, different temperament, different metrics. Borrow the parts that fit, leave the rest.
- **Invest in repeatability.** A good machine that runs for a decade beats a great launch followed by entropy.

Mature isn't a synonym for declining. It's a synonym for established. The job is to keep the machine running and the customer winning. That's the work.
