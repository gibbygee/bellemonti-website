---
title: Software is geological
---

Software isn't a product you stamp out. It accumulates. Every line of code shipped is still there, still running, still needing maintenance, until someone decides to remove it. Each feature lives in relationship to every other feature ever shipped.

<div class="binder-quote">
  "The hard part isn't designing the thing. The hard part is building it ten thousand times without going bankrupt."
  <cite>Elon Musk on manufacturing</cite>
</div>

That constraint — the hard physical limit of production — doesn't exist in software. So we keep building. The maintenance load compounds like debt, not like interest. Year five you have 30 engineers and only 12 of them are building anything new. The rest are holding the walls up.

## The accumulation principle runs wider

- **Organizational complexity** — Every process added is another to maintain. Every layer of management is another layer to route decisions through.
- **Snowflakes** — One-of-a-kind custom implementations. Always increase complexity, always increase maintenance, always handbrake scalability. A one-way ticket.
- **Culture debt** — Same compounding logic. Fragile things accumulate too.

The lesson isn't that accumulation is bad. It's that everything accumulates, whether you intend it to or not. The question is whether you're building toward something durable or toward something fragile.
